# Clawback
## What Is a Clawback?

A clawback is a contractual provision by which money previously paid to an employee must be returned to an employer or [benefactor](/benefactor), here and there with a penalty.

Many companies use clawback policies in employee contracts for motivator based pay like bonuses. They are most frequently utilized in the [financial industry](/finra). Most clawback provisions are non-negotiable. Clawbacks are normally utilized in response to offense, outrages, poor performance, or a drop in company profits.

## Figuring out Clawbacks

Following the financial crisis of 2008, clawback provisos have become more normal since they permit a company to recuperate impetus based pay from CEOs on the off chance that there is unfortunate behavior or any errors in the company's financial reports.

Clawbacks are likewise written into employee contracts so employers have some control over bonuses and other motivating force based payments. The clawback acts as a form of insurance in case the company needs to answer a crisis like fraud, wrongdoing, or on the other hand in the event that the company sees a drop in profits. The employee must likewise pay back monies assuming that the employer feels their performance has been poor.

Clawbacks are not the same as different refunds or repayments since they frequently accompany a penalty. All in all, an employee must pay extra funds to the employer in case the clawback is put into effect.

Clawback provisions prevent individuals from utilizing inaccurate information and are utilized to put a balance between community improvement and corporate welfare. For instance, they can assist with preventing the abuse of accounting information by employees in the financial industry.

Clawbacks are viewed as an important part of the business model since they help to reestablish the confidence and faith of investors and the public in a company or industry. For instance, banks carried out clawback provisions following the financial crisis as a method for revising any future slip-ups by their executives.

## Special Considerations
### Clawbacks and Executive Compensation

The principal federal statute to take into consideration clawbacks of executive pay was the [Sarbanes-Oxley Act](/sarbanesoxleyact) of 2002. It accommodates clawbacks of bonuses and other motivating force based compensation paid to CEOs and CFOs if wrongdoing with respect to the company — not really the actual executives — drives it to rehash financial performance.

The Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008, which was revised the next year, takes into consideration clawbacks of bonuses and motivating force based compensation paid to an executive or the next 20 most generously compensated employees. It applies in cases where financial outcomes are found to have been wrong, whether or not there was any offense. The law just applies to companies that received Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) funds.

In July 2015, a proposed Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) rule associated with the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010 would permit companies to claw back [incentive-based compensation](/accounting-based-motivator) paid to executives in the event of an accounting restatement. The clawback is limited to the excess of what might have been paid under the rehashed results. The rule would require stock exchanges to forbid companies that don't have such clawback provisions written into their contracts from listing. This rule presently can't seem to be approved.

## Clawbacks in Private Equity

The term clawback can likewise be found in a few different settings. In [private equity](/privateequity), it alludes to the limited partners' right to recover part of the general partners' carried interest, in cases where subsequent losses mean the general partners received excess compensation.

Clawbacks are calculated when a fund is liquidated. Medicaid can claw back the costs of care from deceased patients' [estates](/changed gross-domain). At times, clawbacks may not even allude to money — lawyers can claw back privileged reports unintentionally turned over during electronic discovery.

## Instances of Clawback Provisions

A few proposed and enacted federal laws permit clawbacks of executive compensation in light of fraud or accounting errors. Companies may likewise compose clawback provisions into employee contracts, regardless of whether such provisions are required by law, so they can reclaim bonuses that have previously been paid out.

There are numerous instances of clawbacks utilized by corporations, insurance companies, and the federal government. Below are probably the most common clawback provisions put into place today:

- **Executive compensation**: Clawbacks can be utilized if an executive penetrates an agreement, abuses information, or goes to work for a contender.
- **Life insurance**: A provision could direct that the [policy](/lifeinsurance) is canceled and payments should be returned.
- **Dividends**: These can be clawed back under particular conditions.
- **Government contracts**: [Contractors](/self employed entity) might be subject to clawbacks in the event that a few requirements of the contract are not met.
- **Medicaid**: Medicaid can recuperate any monies paid to care for a Medicaid beneficiary after they have passed on.
- **Pensions**: Companies can claw back pensions in the event that there is any evidence of fraud or abuse of information by the beneficiary.
