# Close
## What Is the Close?

The close is a reference to the furthest limit of a [trading session](/tradingsession) in the financial markets when the markets close for the afternoon. The close can likewise allude to the most common way of leaving a [trade](/trade) or the last methodology in a financial transaction wherein contract reports are marked and recorded.

## Grasping the Close

Monitoring when markets open and close is essential for efficient trading, no matter what the exchange or type of security. Knowing when and how to close out a trade is likewise critical for market participants.

The most noticeable illustration of a market close is the close of the [New York Stock Exchange](/nyse) (NYSE) while the closing bell is rung, yet closing times differ among markets and [exchanges](/exchange).

## Common Hours

NYSE equity trading hours are from 9:30 a.m. Eastern Time to 4:00 p.m. Eastern Time. [Pre-market](/premarket) hours start at 6:30 a.m. Eastern Time, while [after-hours](/afterhourstrading) trading closes at 8:00 p.m. Eastern Time. The bond markets will more often than not be open a bit longer from 8:00 a.m. Eastern Time to 5:00 p.m. Eastern Time. Futures market hours shift widely founded on the exchange and item — traders ought to see the exchanges' sites for additional subtleties.

## Closing Prices

The closing price is the price of the last trade before the close of the trading session. These prices are important in light of the fact that they are utilized to make traditional line stock charts, as well as while computing [moving averages](/movingaverage) and other [technical indicators](/technicalindicator).

Since closing prices are widely followed, they might be controlled by fraudulent traders to show up of a rally. This practice, known as "high close" is particularly prevalent with [micro-cap stocks](/microcapstock) that have limited liquidity since less dollar volume is expected to move the price higher.

Traders ought to be careful about involving closing prices as a check of miniature cap and small-cap stock victories and take a gander at [candlestick](/candlestick) charts and different indicators for added knowledge.

## After Hours

Many markets have after-hours trading, which empowers investors to place orders after the close of the trading session. While this might entice, there are several disadvantages that investors ought to consider before trading in after-hours sessions.

The primary downsides to consider include:

- **Limited Liquidity**: Fewer traders are active in after-hours trading, and that means that there's less [liquidity](/liquidity), inefficient pricing, and higher [bid-ask spreads](/bid-askspread).
- **Professional Competition**: Most after-hours traders are professional traders working for [hedge funds](/hedgefund) or investment banks, which makes it hard to contend.
- **No Guarantees**: There is no guarantee that after-hours prices mirror a security's opening price the next day since they are completely various sessions.

Most traders ought to avoid after-hours trading except if they have a ton of experience and a convincing motivation to trade after the close.
