# European Banking Authority (EBA)
## What Is the European Banking Authority (EBA)?

The European Banking Authority (EBA) is a regulatory body that endeavors to keep up with financial stability all through the [European Union's (EU)](/europeanunion) banking industry. It was laid out in 2010 by the European Parliament, supplanting the Committee of European Banking Supervisors (CEBS).

## The Basics of the European Banking Authority (EBA)

The EBA is entrusted with creating regulatory technical standards and rules for financial firms in the EU internal market. It regulates lending institutions, investment firms, and credit institutions. The rules accomplishing the following objectives forces are planned:

- Keep up with the integrity of the financial sector.
- Defend public values by guaranteeing market transparency.
- Balance out the financial system.
- Monitor the quality of new instruments issued by institutions.
- Safeguard consumers, investors, and depositors.
- Direct the supervision of financial institutions.

The European Central Bank (ECB) guarantees that banks follow the rules set forward by the EBA, which runs annual transparency activities and stress tests on in excess of 100 EU banks. This includes developing fiscal data on a bank's capital, risk-weighted assets (RWA), recorded profits and losses, market risk, and credit risk. The stress tests that the EBA forces on financial institutions look to decide if every institution would stay dissolvable in the wake of financial emergencies.

These nations thus looked for bailouts from international institutions.

Fiscal austerity measures intended to assist countries with exitting bailout programs have eased back European economic growth. Simultaneously, the presentation of negative interest rates by the ECB and other central banks has crushed banks' edges.

These factors, combined with increased regulation and poor management, have caused stresses over European banking sustainability.
