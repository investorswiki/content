# S&P 500 Futures
## What Are S&P 500 Futures?
S&P 500 futures are a derivative of the [S&P 500 Index](/sp500), a benchmark U.S. stock measure that is based on the share prices of 500 U.S. companies with large market capitalization. Like with futures contracts for commodities, for example, crude oil, the S&P 500 futures permit an investor to trade on a future date, with a price locked in today. From early March 2021 to early March 2022, S&P 500 futures and the S&P 500 Index had a [correlation coefficient](/correlationcoefficient) of 0.599, which indicated that they tracked each other more often than not.
### As a Stock Market Indicator
Investors and analysts track the S&P 500 futures as a gauge of performance on the U.S. stock market after-hours because the S&P 500 Index works just during normal trading hours on weekdays. While the S&P 500 Index is based on the cash price of stocks being traded inside the benchmark, the S&P 500 futures reflects expectations on the future value of the index, which makes it a leading indicator for the U.S. stock market outside normal trading hours.
### As a Form of Investment
Investors and analysts tend to monitor S&P 500 futures closely after the market's close to find clues about where the direction of the stock market may be headed when trading resumes on the next day, especially during times of [volatility](/volatility), and they can implement trades that complement their strategy, like hedging. A positive return for futures could indicate a higher opening from the previous close for the market, and conversely, a negative return could indicate a lower opening. Futures are widely followed during times of news events that could affect investor sentiment. A strong quarterly earnings report from Apple released after the end of normal trading hours or an economic indicator highlighting strong growth released prior to the stock market's beginning of trading could push futures higher.
On the off chance that, in the morning, futures are trading a lot higher than the cash S&P 500, institutional investors could sell the futures and buy the underlying stocks, giving stocks a lift at the market's open. If, then again, futures aren't trading a lot higher than the S&P 500's close, stocks can go down.
## How Might I Invest in S&P 500 Futures?
The most well known way for individual and institutional investors alike to invest in S&P 500 futures is through the CME Group's E-mini S&P 500 futures, which trades under the ticker symbol ES on the futures and [options](/putoption) trading platform CME Globex. Smaller investors might have to open a special edges or futures account to trade. The E-mini is one fifth the size of standard S&P futures contracts, which are generally used by institutional investors.
There is likewise a futures contract related to the tracking of the volatility of the market, and that is the S&P 500 VIX Mid-Term Futures Index, which measures the return of a daily rolling long position in the fourth, fifth, sixth and seventh month [VIX](/vix) futures contracts.
What's more, there are options available for trading on the E-mini S&P 500.
## How Are S&P 500 Futures Calculated?
The contract unit for the E-mini S&P 500 is $50 times the value of the S&P 500 Index. For example, assuming the underlying S&P 500 Index were trading at the 4,000-point level, the E-mini's contract would be $200,000. There are four quarterly contracts — March, June, September, and December — and they are listed for nine consecutive quarters and three extra December contract months.
## What Are Trading Hours for S&P 500 Futures?
The E-mini S&P 500 futures trades from Sunday, starting at 6 p.m. ET, until Friday, ending at 5 p.m. There is a maintenance period each day from Monday through Thursday between 5 and 6 p.m. Its nearly 24 hours of trading gives global investors access to the futures during a significant part of the week of work. Trading on quarterly contracts ends at 9:30 a.m. ET on the third Friday of the contract month.

## FAQ
### Why Are S&P 500 Futures Widely Followed?
Outside normal trading hours, investors search for clues about how the market could perform when trading resumes the accompanying business day by monitoring S&P 500 futures, especially during times of volatility. Investors likewise can trade S&P 500 futures as a hedge against the index as part of a hedging strategy.
### What Is the Ideal Time for Trading S&P 500 Futures?
A couple of hours before the market opens at 9:30 a.m. during the weekdays may be the ideal time for trading as this is when trading tends to be the most active, giving investors time to change in accordance with events that transpired overnight and to account for what could happen prior to the beginning of trading.
### What Is the Difference Between the S&P 500 Index and S&P 500 Futures?
The S&P 500 Index is based on 500 companies whose combined market capitalization accounts for around 80 percent of the total value of the U.S. stock market, while S&P 500 futures is a derivative based on the underlying value of the benchmark.
