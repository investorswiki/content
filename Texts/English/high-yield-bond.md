# High-Yield (Junk) Bonds
## What Is a High-Yield Bond?
High-yield bonds are[ corporate bonds](/corporatebond) that offer high rates of interest in exchange for your investment dollars, however along with the potential for delicious returns come many risks. High-yield[ bonds](/bond) are issued by companies that are judged liable to default, and that means they could fail to make interest and principal payments on schedule, making the bonds worthless. Consequently, they are otherwise called **junk bonds**.
## What Are the Features of High-Yield Bonds?
High-yield bonds normally . . .
- Have short-term maturities of somewhere in the range of 4 and 10 years.
- Have lower[ credit ratings](/bondrating) than investment-grade bonds, (which we will examine more below).
- Offer a higher yield to make up for the increased risk of default.

High-yield bonds offer yields that are high relative to those of better-quality bonds of comparable maturity. The yield of a junk bond might be higher in light of the fact that it has a greater coupon, or on the grounds that it trades at a lower price.
Interestingly, a high-yield bond wasn't generally conceived that way. A bonds that trades at a price fundamentally below its par value (face value) may not necessarily in all cases have been a high-yield bond. It might have begun as an investment-grade bond, trading nearer to par, and become a high-yield bond as its price dropped, reflecting decay in the issuer's ability to service its debt.
## Who Issues High-Yield Bonds?
As of the finish of 2021, over 15% of the corporate bond market was comprised of high-yield bonds. At that point, this lump of the market was estimated to have around $1.7 trillion in value.
All in all, who normally issues them? High-yield bonds could be issued by companies who have received a credit downgrade yet who need to raise cash fast. These are known as **fallen angels**. Or on the other hand they could emerge out of emerging companies, similar to startups, expecting to secure more capital investment. These are known as **rising stars**.
## How Safe Are High-Yield Bonds?
The platitude goes, "the greater the risk, the greater the reward," however with regards to high-yield bonds, greater yields and possibly higher long-run returns might be offset by their risks. Here are some of them:
- The greatest risk that accompanies high-yield bonds is default risk, the chance that the bond issuer will fail to make interest payments, return the principal, or both.
- All bonds are vulnerable to [interest-rate risk](/interestraterisk), as rising[ interest rates](/interestrate) make the value of bonds decline, and vice versa.
- High-yield bonds experience higher[ volatility](/volatility) than investment-grade bonds or[ Treasury securities](/treasury-securities); truth be told, their price vacillations are more in-line with[ stocks](/commonstock).
- This category of bonds likewise faces [liquidity risk](/liquidity), which is the risk that finding purchasers for an asset could be truly challenging.

## How Are High-Yield Bonds Rated? Who Rates Them?
To assist investors with figuring out the credit quality of bonds, private rating agencies, like Standard and Poor's, Moody's and Fitch Ratings, assess bond issuers at the time they issue a bond. They look at the issuer's ability to make all payments in full and on time, and afterward they distribute their[ bond ratings](/bondrating) in a straightforward grading system, which incorporates a grade and risk level for each bond.

A bond is viewed as high-yield in the event that it conveys a speculative-grade rating, which incorporates grades BB and lower. Be that as it may, a bond isn't left with one grade for eternity. In the event that the issuer's creditworthiness improves, bond ratings agencies upgrade the bonds, and investors can see appreciation as bond value increments.
## How Do You Invest in High-Yield Bonds?
Due to the risk of default, high-yield bonds are generally the domain of large institutional investors. Nonetheless, individual investors can partake of the sector through[ exchange-traded funds (ETFs)](/indexfund) or[ mutual funds](/mutualfund).
Plus, there are[ indices](/marketindex) that track the high-yield bond market, for example,
- S&P U.S. Issued High-Yield Bond Index, and
- FTSE High-Yield Bond Index

Furthermore, at whatever point there's an index, there is likewise an exchange-traded fund that tracks it. A couple of instances of high-yield bond ETFs incorporate the accompanying:
- iShares iBoxx $ High Yield Corporate Bond ETF (HYG)
- iShares Broad USD High Yield Corporate Bond ETF (BATS)
- iShares High Yield Bond Factor ETF (HYDB)

High-yield bond mutual funds incorporate the accompanying:
- RBC BlueBay High Yield Bond Fund (RHYAX)
- Fidelity Capital and Income Fund (FAGIX)
- SEI High Yield Bond Fund (SGYAX)

Any investor who decides to purchase high-yield bonds ought to address any outstanding concerns. Each bond issuer must file a prospectus with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), and each prospectus incorporates the accompanying data:
- The financial condition of the bond issuer
- How the company plans to utilize the proceeds from the sale of the bonds
- The terms of the bond
- The critical risks of investing in the bond

Prospectuses for registered corporate bond offerings are free to the public on the SEC's EDGAR website.
What Are Some Examples of Famous Junk Bonds?
[Inflation](/inflation) was to be faulted for the rise in junk-bond yields to stratospheric levels during the 1980s. With an end goal to combat[ stagflation](/stagflation), a toxic combination of stale growth, rising unemployment, and inflation, the[ Federal Reserve](/federal-reserve) raised the[ discount rate](/discountrate) to 12%. Bond prices fell, however yields on junk bonds soared as high as 14%, thus they turned into the speculative vehicle of decision for lenders behind savings and loans corporations (S&Ls), which were facing inflation-related issues of their own, similar to bankruptcies.
Investor Charles Keating purchased as much as $51 million worth of junk bonds for his S&L, even however it technically carried a net loss of $100 million. Those junk bonds came from Michael Milken's corporation, Drexel Burnham; the two men were sentenced for securities fraud and racketeering and spent time in jail in jail.
