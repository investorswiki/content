# Oversubscription Privilege
## What is an Oversubscription Privilege

An oversubscription privilege gets extended to a company's shareholders on the issuance of a rights or warrants offering. The privilege permits shareholders to purchase any shares staying after different shareholders have had an opportunity to purchase them.

## BREAKING DOWN Oversubscription Privilege

Oversubscription privileges apply to existing shareholders. In a [rights offering](/rightsoffering), a firm generally offers its existing shareholders the right to purchase a specific number shares at a discount to the current share price inside a given time period. Since companies issue shares to fund-raise and an inability to sell all new shares in an issuance could leave a firm [undercapitalized](/undercapitalization), rights issues sometimes utilize a form of contingency plan to deal with shareholders who choose not to exercise their right to purchase new shares. Oversubscription privileges permit shareholders extra rights to purchase a predefined extent of the unexercised shares.

[Oversubscription](/oversubscribed) generally portrays a situation wherein demand exceeds the supply of shares in another issuance. On account of oversubscription privileges, companies assume the oversubscription will happen among the pool of shareholders able to exercise their right to purchase new shares. By and large, this demand originates from shareholders' longing to keep up with their proportional ownership of a firm's shares alongside the voting rights that accompany them. Rights offerings account for this by giving rights and oversubscription privileges in relation to shareholders' current holdings.

## Shareholder Choices During Rights Issues

Companies use rights issues to raise cash from existing shareholders, frequently to pay off existing debts, make a one-time huge scope [capital purchase](/capital-venture) or tackle a cash flow issue. Another share issuance causes [dilution](/dilution), since the bigger number of shares accessible overall reductions the value of some random share as an extent of the whole. Current shareholders seeking to keep up with their proportional holdings need to purchase a number of new shares equivalent to the extent of shares they own. What's more, be that as it may, shareholders need to consider the expected loss of value in their current holdings while concluding whether the discounted price offered for new shares appears to be legit.

Investors ought to likewise investigate the explanations for a rights issuance before practicing those rights. Rights issuances can be an indication of financial difficulty, particularly when companies find themselves incapable to pay down existing debt. Be that as it may, rights offerings don't necessarily show a troubled company. Savvy investors will research the situation to guarantee they have a full image of the benefits and risks implied in purchasing the discounted shares offered in a rights issuance.

As a rule, shareholders offered a rights issuance have three options: they can exercise their rights, overlook their rights and endure a shot from dilution, or, at times, sell the rights to different shareholders or back to an underwriter.
