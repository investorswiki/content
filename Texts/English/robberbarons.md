# Robber Barons
## What Is a Robber Baron?

A robber nobleman is one of America's fruitful [industrialists](/modern transformation) during the nineteenth century, which was otherwise called the Gilded Age. A robber nobleman is a term that is likewise at times credited to any fruitful businessperson whose practices are viewed as deceptive or deceitful. This behavior can incorporate employee or environmental abuse, [stock market manipulation](/manipulation), or purposely confining output to charge higher prices.

## Grasping Robber Barons

The primary known uses of the phrase "robber nobleman" portrayed primitive masters in middle age Europe who looted voyagers, frequently merchant ships along the Rhine River as they passed close by. The term appeared in American papers in 1859. Its modern use originates from Matthew Josephson's **The Robber Barons**.

Robber barons were widely loathed and considered voracious [monopolists](/monopolist) during their lifetimes. Notwithstanding, later memoirs and historical surveys about the Gilded Age's American robber barons cast an additional convoluted and great light.

## Robber Barons and Monopolies

A chief grievance against the nineteenth century business people was that they were monopolists. Fear over the robber barons and their [monopoly](/monopoly) practices increased public support for the [Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890](/sherman-antiturst-act).

Economic theory says a monopolist procures premium profits by confining output and raising prices. This just happens after the monopolist prices out or legally confines any contender firms in the industry. In any case, there is no historical evidence that natural syndications shaped before the Sherman Antitrust Act.

Some supposed robber barons — James J. Slope, Andrew Carnegie, Cornelius Vanderbilt, and John D. Rockefeller — became wealthy entrepreneurs through product innovation and business proficiency. Of the goods and services they gave, supply developed, and prices fell quickly, extraordinarily helping Americans' standards of living. This is something contrary to monopolistic behavior.

## Analysis of Robber Barons

Among common reactions of the early robber barons included poor working conditions for employees, narrow-mindedness, and greed. Some robber barons — including Robert Fulton, Edward K. Collins, and Leland Stanford — earned their wealth through political entrepreneurship.

Numerous wealthy railroad tycoons during the 1800s received privileged access and financing from the government through broad utilization of lobbyists. They received monopolistic special licenses, per-mile endowments, enormous land awards, and low-interest loans.

## Special Considerations

Working conditions in nineteenth century America were trying, without a doubt. While robber barons exploited their workers, they now and again offered better working conditions than the standard of the day. Rockefeller and Ford, for instance, paid higher-than-average wages, including bonuses for innovation or remarkable production. Managers frequently received long excursions at full pay.

Some [tycoons](/tycoon) rank among the most noted [philanthropists](/charity) ever. Rockefeller gave around 10% of each and every paycheck he at any point earned. He gave nearly $550 million to charity and advocated biomedical research, public disinfection, medical training, and educational opportunities for disadvantaged minorities.

Railroad tycoon James J. Slope publicized and gave free education about crop diversification, along with free seed grain, cows, and wood to nearby networks. He would ship migrants at decreased rates on the off chance that they vowed to farm close to his rail lines.
